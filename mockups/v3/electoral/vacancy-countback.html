Elections · a vacant seat

A seat opened up — the old ballots decide again

Renata Silva resigned seat 4 of the New York County legislature. No new election is needed yet: the game re-runs the prior election's ballots with her removed as a candidate (a "vacancy countback"). The voters' original preferences decide the replacement — only if those ballots run out does a special election follow.

Vacancies are filled by re-counting the prior ballots, not by a new election.

The re-run

Re-run source: the prior 2030 New York County election — 287,540 valid ballots, 9 seats, 28,755 votes needed to win a seat (the quota). The count strikes the vacated member and continues from the voters' next preferences. Every prior ballot counts.

Gold tick = the quota. A continuing candidate who reaches it fills the seat; ballots with no remaining preference drop out of the count.

Re-counts the prior ballots with the vacated member removed (see ledger #6).

Branch: winner found

The re-count yields a replacement. The election board certifies, the winner is seated by oath, and committee proportionality is re-checked.

Branch: the ballots run out

No continuing candidate can reach the quota — the re-count fails and a special election must be held no sooner than 90 and no later than 180 days after the vacancy.

Special election window: 90–180 days after the vacancy.

Window for this vacancy: 2031-08-26 → 2031-11-24 (declared 2031-05-28). Dates outside the window are rejected.

About this surface

What this screen does: it walks the vacancy re-count, with its failure branch into a special election; seating hands off to a committee proportionality re-check. The declaration arrives from legislature oversight.

How a vacancy moves: Detected → Declared → Re-count running → Filled or Re-count failed → Special election scheduled (90–180 days) → Filled. The strip above tracks the live record; toggle "countback failed" in the mockup controls to walk the failure branch.
