Judiciary

New York State judiciary

An independent, appointed court serving New York and its constituent counties. Created by supermajority act of the state legislature; judges nominated by the constituent counties in equal numbers and confirmed by legislative consent vote.

Appointed is the default kind of court — converting it to an elected court takes two separate supermajorities.

How this court was created

Judiciary Creation Act 2027-02 — passed 213 of 272 serving members (supermajority required: 182 of 272) · New York State legislature · effective 2027-09-01.

Supermajority: two thirds of all serving members — counted against everyone holding a seat, never just those present.

Two thirds of 272 serving members rounds up to 182. Applies to state law and below.

Nomination — equal numbers from every constituent jurisdiction

Each of the 62 constituent counties of New York nominated exactly one candidate — equal numbers are mandatory. Westchester County declined to nominate, so the legislature's judicial committee supplied the nomination in its stead (the constitutional fallback).

The county-level bench — the New York County court, where most of the demo docket sits — was seated by the same machinery: New York County has no constituent jurisdictions in the dataset, so its judicial committee supplied every nomination (the same fallback).

Constituent jurisdictions nominate in equal numbers; the judicial committee fills in when one declines.

Conversion to an elected judiciary

Converting this appointed court to a directly elected one needs two independent supermajorities: the state legislature, and the constituent counties themselves. Neither alone is enough.

If conversion passes, judges are thereafter elected in groups of at least 5 via STV, and judicial terms sync to the general election clock.

STV: single transferable vote — a proportional ranked-choice count where surplus votes transfer fractionally so no ballot is wasted.

Needs a supermajority of the legislature plus a supermajority of the constituent jurisdictions. Elected judges run in groups of at least 5 per race, with terms synced to the election clock.

Term length — judicial appointments

10 years per judicial appointment Set at founding · must stay in lockstep with civil appointments (also 10 years)

Judicial and civil appointment lengths move together — a legislative act changing one changes both. Renewals re-run the nomination and consent process.
