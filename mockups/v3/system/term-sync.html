The lockstep timeline

All three elected branches in the New York County stack run on the same term, certified 2030-11-01 → expiring 2035-11-01. Appointed officers run on their own 10-year clocks and are shown for contrast — appointment terms are deliberately decoupled from election cycles.

Stylized stand-in — production renders this timeline from the term registry (WF-SYS-01).

Jurisdictions activate independently as their player populations reach critical mass (CLK-06), so clocks start staggered — a county's first term can begin years before its neighbors'. Lockstep alignment is the end-state: harmonizing election days toward a shared general-election day is an encompassing-level normalization that arrives as participation grows.

One clock, every trigger

The general election interval is the single source: legislative terms define it, elected executive and elected judicial terms equal it, and the scheduler derives every election trigger from it. Officials never touch the calendar.

Vacancies never reset the clock

A countback or special-election winner serves the remainder of the term — New York County's seat 4 replacement will still face the voters on 2035-11-01 with everyone else. Special elections fit inside the lockstep; they never move it.

What the engine refuses to do

  • Skip, delay, or reschedule a derived election — there is no API for it; the trigger fires from CLK-01 regardless of who holds office.
  • Extend any elected term past the common expiry, including under active emergency powers — elections cannot be disrupted. Art. II §7
  • Let executive or judicial terms drift from the legislative term when an office converts to elected. Art. III §3; Art. IV §3

Attempted violations are rejected pre-commit and recorded · audit chain · WF-SYS-04
