Organizations · ownership

Transfers and conversions

Ownership moves four ways: by mutual agreement between private parties, by legislative acquisition of a monopoly, by conversion between public and private forms, or by dissolution. Structures can also restructure within the private side by owner consent. Only the monopoly path ever overrides owner consent — and it carries a protected compensation floor.

Mutual consent is the rule; legislative monopoly acquisition is the sole exception.

Mutual transfer Ownership Transfer Initiation

Available to: Organization Agent · Creates: a transfer agreement record (requires mutual consent).

Mutual consent is mandatory for standard transfers. No private acquisition proceeds unless both parties agree — there is no hostile path through this form. The completed record syncs across jurisdictions with full faith & credit.

Compensation

Protected floor · enforced in code, not by policy

Shareholders must be compensated at no less than fair market price. The tendered offer stands at 1.08× the independent appraisal — above the floor, so the engine permits the conversion to proceed.

Compensation can never fall below fair market price.

After conversion: Cobalt Grid becomes a Common Good Corporation; its five sitting directors are each offered founding seats on the new Board of Governors. Its 510 workers keep their co-determination position — 2 worker seats on today's scale. The prior board is offered founding governor seats.

CGC Reorganization/Sale Vote

Vote to reorganize, dissolve, or sell a Common Good Corporation — the public→private direction runs through the legislature, on the open market.

Available to: Legislative Representative · Modifies: the CGC record → reorganized, dissolved, or privatized.

Irreversibility: public-domain IP stays public. Everything a CGC produced while public entered the public domain — and remains there forever. A buyer acquires the enterprise, never the right to re-enclose IP that was already public. A CGC's intellectual property stays in the public domain forever.

Internal restructuring

Ownership-structure conversions within the private side — sole proprietor ↔ partnership ↔ equal partnership ↔ member-owned ↔ stock (opening up stock, for example) — proceed by owner consent under the current structure's own rules. Distinct from the public ↔ private interchange above: no legislature is involved, and the organization stays private throughout.

The consent threshold comes from the current structure's own rules — this partnership agreement requires unanimity; a stock structure would count voting shares instead.
12 of 12 owners consent on record threshold: unanimity (the structure's own rule)

Owner consent follows the structure's own rules — the organization remains private throughout, and structure history is preserved on the public record.

About this surface

What this screen does: it handles the four ways ownership can move — mutual transfer between private parties, monopoly acquisition through the legislature, conversion between public and private forms, and dissolution — plus internal restructuring within the private side.

How ownership state changes: this surface drives the Transfer-Pending → Transferred, Converted Public↔Private, and Dissolved states.

Protected rules here: compensation can never fall below fair market on a monopoly acquisition; a CGC's intellectual property stays in the public domain forever. Both are enforced in code, not by policy.
